What to verify before ordering

  1. Chest width and body length. Compare the listing chart with a garment that already fits.
  2. Fabric weight and composition. Material descriptions and thickness affect comfort, shape and shipping cost.
  3. Print or embroidery placement. Use straight warehouse angles and close-ups to inspect alignment.
  4. Seams and visible defects. Check collars, cuffs, hems, pockets, stains and pulls.
  5. Estimated packaged weight. Heavy jackets and oversized clothing can change parcel efficiency.
Independent research notice: Product listings, sellers, prices, agent services and shipping routes change. Recheck current information before payment.

Build a clothing haul around evidence

Start with a purpose rather than adding every low-priced garment. Compare sizing compatibility, colors, parcel weight and the number of uncertain items. A smaller set of measured, well-verified products is easier to inspect and ship than a large cart built from weak evidence.

Clothing Spreadsheet FAQ

What should a clothing spreadsheet include?

A working source link, current price, variant, size chart, useful QC evidence, weight estimate and last-check date.

Do spreadsheet links guarantee quality?

No. They organize discovery. Use current warehouse photos and exact measurements for the order decision.

Why should I ignore the letter size?

You should not ignore it completely, but measurements are more reliable because seller sizing systems differ.

Can old QC photos still help?

They provide context, but current photos of the same listing and variant are stronger evidence.

What makes clothing expensive to ship?

Heavy fabric, jackets, bulky packaging and a large parcel can increase actual or volumetric weight.
